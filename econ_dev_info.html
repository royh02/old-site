Economic Indices

The numbers politicans go on about are "economic indices." There are plenty of indices to look at, and they all give a glimpse into economic development. There are many other factors, many of which are difficult to explain in a general manner, these indices are often used to approximate development rates. We will look at 2 of the largest

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

When we talk GDP, we usually talk GDP Growth.

Fast growing GDP can mean:

  1. Healthy economy
  1. Increase in employment
  1. Increase in per capita (per person) income

Another important factor in considering GDP is purchasing power. Purchasing power is the value of a currency expressed in terms of the amount of goods or services that one unit of money can buy2^22﻿. A great way to put this in perspective is the Big Mac Index. Because the Big Mac (yes, the 🍔 burger 🍔!) is a very standard item across the globe, it can be used to show how much a given currency can buy compared to another.

Here, we see that ￥20 Chinese Yuan can buy a Big Mac, which costs $5 US Dollars. If we work out the math using the exchange rate:

So a Big Mac can be bought for the equivalent of just $3.13 in China, which is a lot cheaper than in the US ($5)! This means that the purchasing power of the Yuan is greater than that of the US Dollar

The difference in purchasing power is known as purchasing power parity (PPP). Economists like to use GDP with PPP factored in today to analyze economic situations because it reflects the standard of living and economic productivity in a way that can be easily compared. Gini Coefficient

There is a huge flaw with looking at such a generalized index like the GDP — it is an average. This means that it forgets inequality in income. The popular saying the rich get richer, and the poor gets poorer is exactly what the Gini Coefficient looks at.

When inequality is factored in, it helps to see how economic development and growth will translate into betterment of each income bracket. For instance, high growth in a country with low Gini would mean the poor greatly benefits. However, high growth in a country with high Gini would mean the rich reaps most of the benefits while the situation of the poor does not improve by much relatively. As seen in the graph, we see that in the US, there is a disproportional growth in the upper 10% compared to the other 90% that is very evident of inequality. Thus, the increase in GDP only reflects the development of the few elites. In the post-recession period (2009-2012) as shown in the green box, the 90% suffered the most during economic downturns. This is an example of how inequality provides a deeper layer of understanding into the plain GDP values.
